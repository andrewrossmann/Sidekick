Answers to planning questions (KickStarter / rewards)

Working notes for Deborah · Internal use · Figures are planning ranges until BOM and legal sign-off

Below are practical answers to the questions raised in the SideKick Next notes, using standard crowdfunding practice plus Sidekick’s own planning numbers (e.g. indicative MSRP ~$80–$130, COGS ~$20 at target volume) from current materials. Always confirm with counsel and a fulfillment advisor before a public campaign.

1. What does it take to set up a KickStarter campaign — fees? Legal? Best approach?

Campaign and fees

  • Platform (Kickstarter): If your project is successfully funded, Kickstarter collects roughly 5% of the total amount raised, plus payment processing (on the order of 3–5% when card fees and PledgeManager-style flows are included — use Kickstarter’s current fee page for the exact %).
  • Before launch: Clear story, video, images, risk section, budget, delivery timeline, and realistic reward tiers. Many teams also use a pledge manager after the campaign (add-on order fees are separate; budget for that tool).
  • Best approach for hardware: Frame the campaign as pre-orders for a specific product (or version) with defined ship windows — not open-ended “donations” (except for pure awareness, which is unusual for a consumer device).

Legal

  • Entity choice (e.g. LLC) if not already; tax ID; sales tax / nexus planning for the jurisdictions you will ship to.
  • Product claims and labeling: A mobility or balance product may fall under CPSC, FDA, or state rules depending on how it’s classified and what you claim. A short consult with a regulatory or product attorney is appropriate before promising medical benefits.
  • IP: Patent-pending is fine in materials if applications are on file; don’t over-claim.

2. Donations vs. take orders? Estimated price?

Take orders (reward-based commitments) is the right model: backers are buying a product (or a defined bundle) at a stated price. “Donation” is misleading for a product campaign.

Estimated public price (planning): Your materials already use MSRP in the $79.99–$129.99 range. Campaign early-bird / Kickstarter pricing is often 10–25% below eventual MSRP to reward backers; lock margin after COGS, fees, and shipping.

3. Size / color / accessories? Add-ons and future purchases?

  • Keep SKUs small for a first run: e.g. one or two height / frame configurations (if the design allows), 1–2 colors, then accessories as add-ons (tray, cupholder, bag, hooks) and “Step and Carry” style options when ready.
  • Plan post-campaign sales via your own site and/or retail; a pledge manager captures add-on revenue after the campaign closes.

4. Shipping configuration — folded? In pieces for home assembly?

  • Prefer mostly or fully assembled for safety, support burden, and reviews — many competitors ship foldable units that require minimal or no user assembly. If some assembly is required, include a video + print steps and a single hardware kit; “tool-free” is a strong message.
  • Pack for smallest shippable box that protects poles and base; list dim/weight for international quotes.

5. Return policy?

Kickstarter is not a store; you still need a clear policy for defects vs. change of mind by jurisdiction. Typical pattern: warranty for defects / wrong item; limited window for unopened returns if you offer that; document restocking or return shipping. Have counsel align policy with where you will sell and ship.

6. Budget items (checklist from your list)

Your categories map cleanly to a simple budget:

  • Marketing & advertising — pre-launch, campaign ads, and post-campaign email.
  • Legal — entity, terms, product claims, basic IP and supplier contracts.
  • Fees — platform %, payment processing, pledge manager, possibly currency conversion.
  • Parts (BOM) — lock with CM; your planning COGS is ~$20; verify at chosen volume and spec.
  • Labor / employees — what kind? Often for a first run: founders + a contract project manager, engineer, and logistics coordinator rather than a large payroll; add customer support part-time for campaign week.
  • Shipping (including packaging) — boxes, padding, label systems, 3PL if used.
  • Insurance — general liability, product liability once you sell, and any cargo/warehouse as needed; ask a broker for hardware consumer products.

7. Review of existing materials: video, pics, drawings, text

Yes, review and refresh before launch so one consistent story, current prototype photos, a single tagline, and a short risks section. The team already has a public photo album and narrative docs; align campaign copy with the same facts and no conflicting older codenames (unless you briefly explain the product name evolution).

8. Do we need an instruction manual? What should be included?

Yes for a foldable, wheeled, multi-part device. Include: safety warnings; parts diagram; open / fold / lock; brake / roll behavior in plain language; care and max weight; customer support contact; and QR to a how-to video. A one-page “quick start” plus a fuller PDF works well.

9. Assembly?

State honestly: e.g. “unfold, lock height, attach accessories as shown.” If true, “no tools” is a differentiator. Match what manufacturing can repeat every time.

10. How much in-house vs. farming out?

Common balance: you keep product definition, IP, quality criteria, and backer comms in-house; you farm out tooling, volume manufacturing, and often fulfillment to qualified partners. Design validation and test plan can use outside labs for aspects you don’t run in-house.

11. Testing

Build a test plan: key mechanical cycles (fold, roll, brake), load / stability, packaging drop tests as appropriate, and a small user try-out cohort with documented feedback. Tie tests to the claims you’ll make publicly.

Overall suggestions (for Deborah and the team)

  • Pre-launch — Lock BOM and ship dates conservatively; under-promise on timing.
  • Regulatory and claims — One lawyer session on how we describe the product in campaign copy.
  • Single project owner for campaign + fulfillment checklist so questions like yours roll into a dated decision log.
  • Backer support — Template responses for “where’s my unit,” damage, and assembly; assign coverage during the funding spike.

This is guidance, not legal, tax, or medical advice. Confirm fees and terms on Kickstarter’s site, and have counsel review campaign text and your return and warranty copy before you go live.
